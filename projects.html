Python Telegram Attendance Bot

Status: Completed

As a clerk in National Service, one of my jobs was to collate the attendances of everyone in office every day. When we did it manually, it took a lot of time, as we had to hunt down our superiors, and await their replies of attendance slowly. At this time, I had just learned Python, and decided this would be the first practical project I could do. This bot is designed to message all users at a specific time for attendance, and continue to message them if they do not reply.

I did this before I learned of CSV files, so I reinvented the wheel in making a file text format for persistent memory, a rather fragile database of 9 lines of data per person separated by newlines. In future projects, I'll instead use better database systems such as SQL or CSV, but I'm still proud of what I managed without the awareness of such systems

Python code for reading into dictionary

                            
for line in procstr.splitlines():
    goodline = line.strip()
    cycle = count % 9
    if cycle == 0:  #user id
        userbase[goodline] = []
        currentuser = goodline
    if cycle in range(1,5) or cycle == 8:  #strings
        userbase[currentuser].append(goodline)
    if cycle in range(5,8):  #boolean
        if goodline == 'True':
            userbase[currentuser].append(True)
        else:
            userbase[currentuser].append(False)
    count += 1

Lessons Learnt

I learned a lot from this project. Many problems in our world have very simple solutions, we just have not realised that there are solutions with what tools we have. Take the wheel for example. In hindsight, it was a very simple idea. But at the time, only a few had what was needed to devise it in their minds. This realization humbled me and gave me respect for other ideas and applications I found boring, or looked down on.

It also taught me the value of my work. I gave the program to my office for free, and hosted it on my own computer 24/7. This was because I thought such a simple, lightweight program wasn't worth charging for. The many days spent educating them on usage or on bugfixing or creating improvements have taught me otherwise. It also gave me an appreciation for open source programs being maintained by others, free of charge. While this doesn't mean I will charge for my work now onwards, it means I will properly value my own work as well, and not downplay my own worth.

Adapting the program, which used to run locally on a Raspberry Pi, for usage with the Heroku system also taught me much about adapting to others' frameworks and utilising them well.

3D Printer

Status: In progress

My first 3D printer was a Da Vinci MiniMaker, given to me for free. However, I was very frustrated with it, as the company had prevented the usage of other filaments with NFC tags as well as locked the 3d printer to a painfully limied, proprietary Model Slicer (a program to convert 3D models into instructions for the machine). Hence, I quickly got a new, more open-source printer, and tore the Da Vinci printer apart, removing all proprietary elements to rebuild it anew, so that It would play well with 3rd party filaments and software.

I had to model new pieces for the printer to accomodate for open source parts, whose designs were different as compared to their proprietary complements. The wiring, power supply and control board were all replaced by myself as well, as even the most common 3D printing hardware today far outshines the original parts from 6 years ago.

Lessons Learnt

As each new part was individually sourced, ironically, as compared to the replacement 3D Printer I bought, which is still more reliable than my revamped Da Vinci printer, this custom printer was far more expensive to make. A real life experience of economies of scale - one custom device is likely much more expensive per unit as compared to mass produced versions of it. I also gained some experience with 3D modeling, editing as well as experience with electric systems.
